# State FAQ

Plain-language FAQ for the states currently tracked in this repo. This is a tracking aid, not legal advice. Always read the linked legislation notes and primary sources before relying on any item.

Last updated: 2026-07-16.

## California

Source note: [legislation/california.md](legislation/california.md)

**What does California law do?**

California bars anyone other than a state-licensed firearms manufacturer from using a CNC milling machine or 3D printer to manufacture a firearm. It also restricts sale, transfer, possession, purchase, or receipt of CNC machines or 3D printers whose sole or primary function is manufacturing firearms.

**What does the active proposal do?**

AB 2047 would create a future printer-level blocking-technology system. If enacted as tracked, the state would issue performance guidance, manufacturers would submit attestations, and covered noncompliant printers could not be sold or transferred in California after the future compliance date.

**What does it not do?**

The current law does not ban all 3D printers. It targets firearm manufacture and machines whose sole or primary function is manufacturing firearms. The active blocking-technology proposal is not the same thing as current law unless enacted and implemented.

**What should be watched next?**

AB 2047 status, amendments, compliance dates, exceptions, and any California DOJ guidance.

## Colorado

Source note: [legislation/colorado.md](legislation/colorado.md)

**What does Colorado law do?**

HB26-1144 prohibits knowingly manufacturing or producing a potentially functioning firearm, unfinished frame or receiver, large-capacity magazine, or rapid-fire device by 3D printing. The bill treats 3D printing as including additive and subtractive manufacturing.

**Who is carved out?**

The enacted bill includes exceptions for federally licensed firearm manufacturers, accredited gunsmithing programs, and instructors or students in those programs.

**What does it not do?**

It does not ban general-purpose 3D printers. The Colorado bill summary says the prohibition applies to potentially functional firearms and firearm components.

**What should be watched next?**

Implementation, enforcement guidance, and whether Colorado introduces a separate printer-sale or blocking-technology bill later.

## Delaware

Source note: [legislation/delaware.md](legislation/delaware.md)

**What does Delaware law do?**

Delaware law makes it a felony for an unlicensed person to use a 3D printer or similar device to manufacture or produce a firearm, firearm receiver, or major firearm component. It also covers distributing digital instructions to unlicensed persons when those instructions can be used to program a 3D printer for covered firearm production.

**What does the active proposal do?**

HB 399 would require 3D printers sold or delivered in Delaware to include firearm-blocking technology after a feasibility review and implementing regulations.

**What does it not do?**

Current law does not appear to impose a general ban on owning ordinary 3D printers. HB 399 is still a proposal in this tracker and includes feasibility and rulemaking steps before a blocking-technology mandate would operate.

**What should be watched next?**

HB 399 committee movement, feasibility findings, rulemaking language, and final definitions of covered printers.

## Hawaii

Source note: [legislation/hawaii.md](legislation/hawaii.md)

**What does Hawaii law do?**

Hawaii bars a person who is not licensed to manufacture firearms, or who is not a federally licensed dealer, from producing with a 3D printer certain unserialized firearm receivers or parts for the purpose of assembling a firearm. A violation is listed as a class C felony.

**What active proposal is being counted?**

No active direct 3D-printing firearm ban proposal is counted in this filtered tracker. SB 285 was noted, but it was not treated as an active direct 3D-printing ban for this sweep.

**What does it not do?**

The tracked law does not ban all 3D printing. It is tied to producing covered unserialized firearm parts for firearm assembly and to license status.

**What should be watched next?**

Whether SB 285 or a successor ghost-gun bill becomes active and whether it adds direct 3D-printing or digital-file restrictions.

## Maine

Source note: [legislation/maine.md](legislation/maine.md)

**What does Maine law do?**

LD 1126 / Public Law 537 requires covered firearms, frames, receivers, and unfinished frames or receivers to have serial numbers, creates a process for federal firearms licensees to imprint serial numbers, and prohibits undetectable firearms.

**How does it involve 3D printing?**

The chaptered law expressly defines "three-dimensional printer" and defines unfinished frames or receivers to include printing, extrusion, machined bodies, and similar articles that may readily become frames or receivers.

**What does it not do?**

It does not require printer-level blocking technology, and it does not appear to ban ordinary ownership of general-purpose 3D printers.

**What should be watched next?**

Implementation before the January 1, 2027 violation date, FFL serialization processes, and any follow-on bill aimed at digital files or printer-level controls.

## Massachusetts

Source note: [legislation/massachusetts.md](legislation/massachusetts.md)

**What does Massachusetts law do?**

Massachusetts regulates use of 3D printers and CNC milling machines to manufacture or assemble firearms. The tracked statute prohibits use of those machines for firearm manufacture or assembly in Massachusetts without a valid license to carry firearms.

**What else is covered?**

Related provisions restrict sale or transfer of machines that have a primary or intended function of manufacturing or assembling firearms.

**What does it not do?**

It does not appear to be a total ban on ordinary 3D printers. It is narrower than a universal printer ban and is focused on firearm manufacture, assembly, and firearm-manufacturing machines.

**What should be watched next?**

Any state guidance on how the license-to-carry condition is applied, plus any new bills aimed at digital files or printer-level blocking technology.

## Michigan

Source note: [legislation/michigan.md](legislation/michigan.md)

**What does current Michigan law do?**

This tracker has not found a current Michigan state law that directly bans 3D printing firearms in the same way as the enacted-law states listed here.

**What would the active proposal do?**

SB 331 and companion SB 332 would add serialization requirements and prohibit using a 3D printer or CNC milling machine for unlicensed firearm manufacturing.

**What does it not do?**

Because the tracked item is an active proposal, it should not be treated as current law. The tracker also does not treat it as a general ban on owning or using 3D printers for non-firearm purposes.

**What should be watched next?**

House Government Operations activity, substitute text, final passage, and whether the proposal is amended to cover digital files or printer sales.

## Minnesota

Source note: [legislation/minnesota.md](legislation/minnesota.md)

**What does current Minnesota law do?**

This tracker has not found a current Minnesota state law that directly bans 3D printing firearms in the way tracked here.

**What would the active proposal do?**

SF 3661 / HF 3407 would limit 3D printing of firearms to federally licensed firearms manufacturers and prohibit distribution of 3D-printer firearm design files.

**What does it not do?**

The proposal is not counted as enacted law. The House version was tracked as dead in committee, while the Senate version had advanced farther, so the proposal needs status follow-up before being treated as viable or dead.

**What should be watched next?**

Senate file status, any companion reintroduction, amended text, and whether the digital-file restriction survives.

## New Jersey

Source note: [legislation/new-jersey.md](legislation/new-jersey.md)

**What does New Jersey law do?**

New Jersey makes it a crime for an unlicensed person to use a 3D printer or similar device to manufacture or produce a firearm, firearm receiver, magazine, or firearm component. It also restricts distributing digital instructions to unlicensed persons in New Jersey when those instructions may be used to program a 3D printer for covered firearm or component production.

**What did the newer digital-instructions law add?**

A4975 added a crime for possessing digital instructions to illegally manufacture firearms and firearm components.

**What does it not do?**

The tracked law does not ban all 3D printers or all CAD files. It is focused on unlicensed firearm/component production and illegal digital instructions connected to covered firearm manufacture.

**What should be watched next?**

Implementation of the digital-instructions crime, court challenges, and any follow-on proposal aimed at printer-level blocking technology.

## North Carolina

Source note: [legislation/north-carolina.md](legislation/north-carolina.md)

**What would the active proposal do?**

HB 545 / S 409 would ban ghost guns and undetectable firearms. The official North Carolina summary says it would make it a Class I felony to manufacture, sell, give away, transfer, use, or possess a ghost gun or undetectable firearm, with exceptions for licensed firearm manufacturers.

**How does it involve 3D printing?**

The Legislative Reporting Service summary says the proposal defines an undetectable firearm to include a firearm manufactured through a 3D printing process.

**What does it not do?**

It is not a printer-sale blocking-technology bill. This tracker does not treat it as current law unless enacted.

**What should be watched next?**

House Rules movement, companion S 409 movement, amendments, and whether the text is broadened to digital files or printer-level controls.

## New York

Source note: [legislation/new-york.md](legislation/new-york.md)

**What does New York law do?**

The SFY 2026-2027 enacted budget included provisions targeting 3D-printed firearms. The tracked public summaries describe prohibitions on using 3D printers to manufacture ghost guns, silencers, magazines, or other firearm parts, plus restrictions on sale and distribution of digital instructions to build those devices.

**What else did the budget create?**

It created a working-group pathway to assess feasibility of 3D-printer blocking technology. If found feasible and implemented, 3D printers sold in New York could be required to include blocking technology.

**What active proposals are still tracked?**

A2228/S3562 would require background checks for purchases of 3D printers capable of creating firearms. Other active or introduced measures address 3D-printed firearms and digital instructions.

**What does it not do?**

The background-check and related active bills should not be treated as enacted unless they pass. The blocking-technology requirement is tied to feasibility and implementation steps, not an immediate universal printer mandate in this tracker.

**What should be watched next?**

Final chaptered budget text, agency guidance, working-group findings, and movement on background-check bills.

## Rhode Island

Source note: [legislation/rhode-island.md](legislation/rhode-island.md)

**What does Rhode Island law do?**

Rhode Island prohibits manufacturing, selling, offering to sell, transferring, purchasing, possessing, or having under one's control a ghost gun, an undetectable firearm, or any firearm produced by a 3D printing process.

**Who is carved out?**

The tracked statute does not apply to federally licensed manufacturers with ATF Type 07 licenses.

**What does it not do?**

It does not ban all 3D printers or all printed objects. The tracked restriction is tied to ghost guns, undetectable firearms, and firearms produced by a 3D printing process.

**What should be watched next?**

Any statutory amendments that change the definition of 3D printing process, ghost gun, undetectable firearm, or the Type 07 carve-out.

## Virginia

Source note: [legislation/virginia.md](legislation/virginia.md)

**What does Virginia law do?**

HB 40 was enacted in 2026 and restricts unfinished frames or receivers and unserialized firearms. Giffords summarizes the law as restricting importation, purchase, sale, transfer, manufacture, assembly, and later possession of unserialized firearms, frames, and receivers.

**How does it involve 3D printing?**

The introduced bill text defines manufacture or assembly to include additive processes and defines unfinished frames or receivers to include printing, extrusion, machined bodies, and similar items.

**What does it not do?**

It does not require printer-level blocking technology and does not appear to ban ordinary 3D printers as tools.

**What should be watched next?**

Implementation dates in 2027, enforcement guidance, litigation, and any follow-on proposal aimed specifically at 3D-printer sales or digital files.

## Washington

Source note: [legislation/washington.md](legislation/washington.md)

**What does Washington law do?**

HB 2320 regulates firearm manufacturing, including 3D-printed firearms and digital gun blueprints, and strengthens Washington's ghost-gun statute. It became law in 2026.

**What does the active proposal do?**

HB 2321 would require 3D printers to include certain blocking technologies.

**What does it not do?**

HB 2321 is not treated here as current law. HB 2320 is focused on firearms, firearm manufacturing, and digital gun blueprints; it is not a blanket ban on general-purpose 3D printing.

**What should be watched next?**

HB 2321 committee movement, technical definitions of blocking technology, and enforcement guidance under HB 2320.
