# July 16, 2026 Legislative And News Sweep

Fresh sweep for proposed 3D-printing firearm legislation and related news.

This is a status update, not legal advice. Official bill pages control over media summaries when they conflict.

## Bottom Line

The main official legislative movement since the July 8 sweep is **California AB 2047**. The bill remains active in the California Senate and is scheduled for a Senate Appropriations hearing on **August 3, 2026**.

No other tracked state proposal showed new official forward movement in this pass.

The news cycle, however, is active. The Verge, EFF, 3DPrint.com, and other outlets are now framing the fight as a shift from regulating files or finished firearms to regulating the printers themselves through surveillance / print-blocking software.

## News And Commentary Updates

### The Verge, July 7, 2026

The Verge published a long policy/tech feature on California and New York printer-blocking legislation. The article frames the issue as a move from regulating online files toward regulating physical machines through "blueprint scanning" or "print blocker" software.

Key points to carry forward:

- It says Washington HB 2321 was an early model for blocking-technology legislation and describes it as having died in committee.
- It notes California AB 2047 still needs Senate approval and the governor's signature.
- It reports that California's bill would make non-roster printers illegal to sell after December 1, 2029, with civil penalties up to $25,000 per violation.
- It describes the practical split between hash matching and predictive / AI scanning.
- It quotes maker and EFF concerns about false positives, cloud scanning, private file exposure, and right-to-repair impacts.
- It reports that Print&Go's founder said the company is collaborating with Everytown and the New York governor's office.

### EFF, July 14, 2026

EFF published "Don't Repeat NY's 3D Printing Blunder." Its core update is that New York's final law reduced some harms compared with earlier language but still leaves major concerns.

Important EFF points:

- New York dropped the face-to-face sale requirement for 3D printers and CNC machines.
- File-sharing penalties were reduced in some respects, but EFF says ambiguity remains around possession with intent to share.
- EFF argues the working group has no required public transparency and may still produce state-mandated "censorware."
- EFF is using New York as a warning against California following the same path.

### EFF, June 26, 2026

EFF also published a California-specific update after amendments to AB 2047.

Important points:

- EFF says the performance standard was weakened from preventing technically skilled evasion to substantially reducing foreseeable circumvention attempts.
- EFF criticizes the bill for relying on non-governmental third parties and manufacturer / reseller self-policing.
- EFF highlights a commercial-entertainment carve-out and argues it leaves out smaller creators such as indie filmmakers and cosplayers.

### 3DPrint.com, June 17, 2026

3DPrint.com framed New York and California as the two key states shifting toward printer regulation rather than only firearm regulation.

Important points:

- New York's law directs the Division of Criminal Justice Services to lead a task force for standards.
- New York will eventually be able to take action against companies selling non-compliant printers once regulations are in place.
- Critics including David Tobin and EFF question whether printers can reliably identify firearm parts and warn about broad effects on schools, businesses, makers, and lawful users.

### AP / PBS / Police1 Syndication

AP's reporting continues to be the central general-news piece on this issue. Police1 republished the AP coverage with emphasis for law-enforcement audiences.

Important points:

- New York's law could eventually require 3D printers sold for home and business use to include firearm-blocking technology.
- California is considering similar legislation.
- The blocking mandate would not begin until 2029 or later, depending on study / feasibility steps.
- The piece quotes both technical optimism from Physna and skepticism from 3D-printing and civil-liberties critics.
