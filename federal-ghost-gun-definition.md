# Federal Ghost Gun Definition

Plain-language reference for the federal meaning of "ghost gun" as used in this tracker. This is a tracking aid, not legal advice.

Last updated: 2026-07-08.

## Short Answer

Federal law and ATF regulations do not primarily use "ghost gun" as the formal legal term. The closest federal regulatory term is **privately made firearm (PMF)**.

Under 27 CFR 478.11, a **privately made firearm (PMF)** is a firearm, including a frame or receiver, that was completed, assembled, or otherwise produced by someone other than a licensed manufacturer and did not have a serial number placed by a licensed manufacturer when it was produced.

ATF states that PMFs are commonly called "ghost guns" because they can be difficult to trace.

## Core Federal Building Blocks

### Firearm

The federal Gun Control Act definition of "firearm" includes:

- a weapon that will, is designed to, or may readily be converted to expel a projectile by explosive action;
- the frame or receiver of such a weapon;
- a firearm muffler or silencer;
- a destructive device;
- but not an antique firearm.

ATF regulations also state that a firearm includes a weapon parts kit that is designed to, or may readily be completed, assembled, restored, or otherwise converted to expel a projectile by explosive action.

### Frame or Receiver

Federal regulations define "frame" and "receiver" based on the part of the weapon that houses or structures key firing components. The regulations also cover certain partially complete, disassembled, or nonfunctional frames or receivers when they are designed to, or may readily be, completed or converted to function as a frame or receiver.

The regulation says raw material or an unfinished article that has not yet reached the stage where it is clearly identifiable as an unfinished weapon component is not a frame or receiver.

### Privately Made Firearm

A PMF is not just any homemade object. It must still be a firearm, frame, or receiver under the federal definitions. The "privately made" part means it was produced by someone other than a licensed manufacturer and did not receive a licensed manufacturer's serial number at the time it was produced.

## What "Ghost Gun" Usually Means Federally

In federal tracking and ATF materials, "ghost gun" usually means:

- a privately made firearm;
- often unserialized;
- made or assembled outside the licensed manufacturing system;
- difficult for law enforcement to trace through normal firearm tracing records.

The term can include firearms made with 3D printing, but it is not limited to 3D-printed firearms. It can also include firearms made from parts kits, unfinished frames or receivers, or other manufacturing methods.

## What The Federal Definition Does

It gives federal regulators a way to treat covered weapon parts kits, readily convertible firearms, frames, receivers, and certain privately made firearms as firearms for purposes such as:

- licensed manufacturing and dealing rules;
- serialization and marking requirements for licensees;
- background-check and transfer rules for covered commercial sales;
- acquisition and disposition records for federal firearms licensees;
- tracing firearms recovered by law enforcement.

The ATF frame-or-receiver final rule, signed April 11, 2022 and effective August 24, 2022, amended federal regulations to address PMFs, frame-or-receiver definitions, weapon parts kits, marking, and recordkeeping.

In 2025, the U.S. Supreme Court upheld ATF's authority against a facial challenge to regulate some weapon parts kits and unfinished frames or receivers under the Gun Control Act in *Bondi v. VanDerStok*.

## What The Federal Definition Does Not Do

It does not make "ghost gun" the main formal statutory term. The more precise federal regulatory label is PMF.

It does not mean every 3D printer, CNC machine, CAD file, plastic part, raw block of material, or unfinished object is automatically a firearm.

It does not mean all privately made firearms are automatically illegal under federal law. ATF states that an individual may generally make a firearm for personal use, but people engaged in the business of manufacturing firearms for sale or distribution must be licensed, and National Firearms Act restrictions may apply.

It does not erase state law. States may define, restrict, or ban ghost guns, 3D-printed firearms, unserialized firearms, digital firearm files, or printer use more strictly than federal law.

## Tracker Usage

For this repo, "ghost gun" should be treated as a broad public-policy phrase. When precision matters, use:

- **PMF** for the federal regulatory concept;
- **firearm** for the Gun Control Act / 27 CFR firearm definition;
- **frame or receiver** for the regulated core weapon component;
- **3D-printed firearm** only when the law specifically involves 3D printing or additive manufacturing.
