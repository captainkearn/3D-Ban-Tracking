# Physna Financial-Ties Research Note

Research note on whether lawmakers and supporters of 3D-printer firearm-blocking laws are financially tied to Physna.

This page uses "financially tied" in the evidence-based sense: campaign contributions, lobbying payments, contracts, grants, disclosed sponsorships, employment, consulting, equity/investment, or other direct financial relationships. Public agreement with Physna's policy position is not treated as a financial tie by itself.

Last updated: 2026-07-09.

## Short Answer

This pass found **no public evidence that the tracked state lawmakers or the main advocacy supporters are directly financially tied to Physna**.

What this pass did find:

- Physna has a clear business interest in printer-blocking mandates because the laws would create demand for geometric-search / physical-AI compliance systems.
- Physna has publicly promoted itself as relevant to New York and California 3D-printer regulations.
- Physna's LinkedIn page stated that the only way printer manufacturers can meet the New York-style requirement is through "Physical AI and Geometry" and "through Physna."
- AP/PBS coverage quoted a Physna technical account manager saying geometric search is mature, deployed, and ready to apply to this problem.
- FEC data shows federal political contributions by individuals listing Physna as employer, including Physna CEO Paul Powers, but the visible federal recipients found in this pass are not the tracked state lawmakers.
- Physna has venture-capital backing and federal defense/procurement business, but this pass did not connect that money to the state printer-blocking bills.

The best-supported claim right now is **potential regulatory-capture incentive**, not proven pay-to-play.

## Evidence Standard

This page separates four categories:

| Category | Meaning | Status in this pass |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direct financial tie | Money, contract, employment, equity, or lobbying payment between Physna and a lawmaker/supporter | Not found for tracked state lawmakers/supporters |
| Political contribution by Physna-affiliated person | A person listing Physna as employer makes a reported campaign contribution | Found federally, not tied to tracked state lawmakers |
| Policy alignment / public support | Physna or a supporter publicly favors blocking mandates | Found |
| Business incentive | Physna could benefit if mandates create a market for its technology | Strongly plausible |

## What Public Records Show

### Physna's Public Position

Physna describes itself as a software company using proprietary algorithms and geometric deep-learning technology to codify 3D models into software-readable data.

Physna's LinkedIn page recently discussed New York and California 3D-printing regulations, hosted webinars for manufacturers, universities, libraries, and makerspaces, and stated that manufacturers would need physical AI and geometry to meet the requirements.

That matters because it shows a company-side commercial interest in the regulatory problem. It does not, by itself, prove that lawmakers or advocacy groups received money from Physna.

### Physna In Media Coverage

AP/PBS coverage of New York and California printer-blocking laws quoted Julian Chultarsky, identified as a technical account manager at Physna, saying geometric search is mature and ready to be applied to the problem.

The same coverage also quoted Everytown policy advocate Samuel Levy supporting the policy goal and EFF criticizing the technology as likely to overblock lawful speech and raise privacy concerns.

The article places Physna, Everytown, and EFF in the same policy debate, but it does not report a financial relationship between Physna and Everytown or between Physna and any lawmaker.

### California AB 2047

The California bill page lists Assemblymember Rebecca Bauer-Kahan as lead author, with Senator Grayson and Assemblymembers Patel, Rogers, and Stefani as coauthors.

At the June 30, 2026 California Senate Public Safety Committee hearing, Bauer-Kahan said she was presenting AB 2047 with her sponsors, Everytown for Gun Safety. Everytown and Moms Demand Action witnesses testified in support.

This pass found no public evidence that Bauer-Kahan, the listed coauthors, Everytown, Moms Demand Action, or Students Demand Action received money from Physna.

What is documented is policy alignment:

- Everytown is the named sponsor/supporter of AB 2047.
- Physna publicly says its technology is relevant to the type of mandates New York and California are considering.
- Critics at the hearing alleged the technology does not work as described and named Physna as the apparent technology company in the space.

That supports a conflict-of-interest question, not a concluded financial-tie claim.

### Delaware HB 399

Delaware's bill page lists Rep. Griffith as primary sponsor, Rep. Kamela Smith and Sen. Lockman as additional sponsors, and Reps. Morrison and Snyder-Hall plus Sens. Hansen and Pinkney as co-sponsors.

This pass found no public evidence tying those sponsors to Physna financially.

Delaware's bill would require a working group, attorney general standards, manufacturer attestations, and potentially a secure library of banned 3D files. That creates a market opportunity for vendors like Physna, but the bill page itself does not name Physna.

### New York

New York's enacted budget package created the most prominent printer-blocking pathway. New York's separate A2228/S3562 background-check proposal lists Assemblymember Rajkumar as sponsor and Assemblymember Catalina Cruz as co-sponsor.

This pass found no public evidence of a direct financial tie between Physna and Gov. Kathy Hochul, Assemblymember Rajkumar, Assemblymember Cruz, Everytown, or Hispanic Federation.

Physna's public posts specifically discuss New York's regulation and market it as a problem manufacturers need to solve. Again, that shows business interest, not a proven lawmaker payment.

### Washington HB 2321

Washington's HB 2321 bill page lists Rep. Salahuddin as sponsor along with Peterson, Berry, Taylor, Farivar, Reed, Ramel, Santos, Obras, Fitzgibbon, Leavitt, Nance, Callan, Kloba, Thomas, Ryu, Doglio, Gregerson, Ormsby, Berg, Reeves, Macri, Fosse, Bergquist, Hill, and Pollet.

This pass found no public evidence tying those sponsors to Physna financially.

Washington's bill would create a strong compliance market because it calls for blocking features, attorney general standards, attestations, a state database, and anti-circumvention resistance. But the public bill page does not name Physna.

## Federal Political Contributions Found

FEC individual-contribution data shows itemized federal contributions by people listing Physna, Physna Inc., or Physna LLC as employer.

The notable record from this pass:

| Contributor | Employer / occupation listed | Recipient visible in FEC result | Amount pattern | Relevance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Paul Powers | Physna / CEO | Morris for Senate | $7,000 gross with redesignation entries visible in the API result | Federal contribution; not tied to tracked state lawmakers |
| Several Physna employees | Engineering, design, social media, sales roles | ActBlue or WinRed | Mostly small-dollar contributions | Federal giving; not evidence of company payment or bill-specific influence |

Important limits:

- FEC data covers federal committees, not all state campaigns.
- Employer-listed contributions are individual donations, not automatically company donations.
- This pass did not find those donations going to the state lawmakers in the tracker.
- ActBlue and WinRed entries often act as conduits; they do not prove support for a specific state bill.

## Physna Investors And Business Relationships

Physna is venture-backed. Public sources list investors including Drive Capital, Sequoia Capital, Tiger Global, and GV.

Physna also has federal-government-facing business. A GSA eLibrary page lists Physna Inc. as contractor on MAS contract 47QTCA24D00BN, and public APEX / government-contracting materials describe Physna receiving U.S. Air Force SBIR Phase III work related to supply chain and sourcing problems.

This matters because Physna is not a tiny hobby project. It is a venture-backed company with government and enterprise ambitions.

But this pass did not find evidence that those investors or contracts funded the state printer-blocking bills or paid the state lawmakers/supporting nonprofits.

## What This Does Not Prove

This does not prove that Physna paid lawmakers.

This does not prove that Everytown, Moms Demand Action, Students Demand Action, Hispanic Federation, or Alliance for Gun Responsibility received money from Physna.

This does not prove that any lawmaker holds Physna equity.

This does not prove that Physna drafted the bills.

This does not rule out nonpublic communications, future contracts, later lobbying registrations, undisclosed nonprofit support, or state-level campaign contributions not surfaced in this pass.

## What The Evidence Does Support

The evidence supports a narrower and stronger claim:

> Physna has a direct commercial incentive for states to mandate 3D-printer blocking technology, and it is publicly positioning itself as a solution provider for those mandates. Public records reviewed so far do not show a direct financial tie between Physna and the tracked state lawmakers or advocacy supporters.

That is still an important story. A company does not need to pay a lawmaker directly to benefit from a law that creates a market for its product.

## Working Conclusion

The current public evidence is enough to ask hard questions about **regulatory capture**:

- Is a private vendor shaping the standard?
- Did any lawmaker or agency consult Physna before bill language was drafted?
- Will the resulting rules effectively require Physna-like technology?
- Will competitors be able to comply?
- Will the state publish vendor-neutral, independently tested standards?
- Who gets the contract or licensing revenue if mandates go live?

But the current public evidence is **not enough** to claim that named lawmakers or advocacy groups are financially tied to Physna.

The clean public statement is:

> No direct financial tie has been found yet between Physna and the tracked lawmakers/supporters. The documented issue is a market-incentive and regulatory-capture concern: Physna publicly presents its technology as a solution to laws that would create a compliance market.
