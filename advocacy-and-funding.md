# Advocacy And Funding Behind 3D-Printed Firearm Legislation

Research note on who is publicly pushing, supporting, organizing, or funding the advocacy ecosystem behind state 3D-printed firearm and ghost-gun legislation tracked in this repo.

This page is an evidence ledger, not a legal conclusion. A group can support a bill without directly funding it, and a donor can fund an advocacy organization without earmarking money for a specific bill. The notes below distinguish those things where the public record allows.

Last updated: 2026-07-08.

## Bottom Line

The clearest, best-documented national driver behind the recent state push is the **Everytown for Gun Safety ecosystem**:

- **Everytown for Gun Safety Action Fund**, a 501(c)(4), does advocacy and legislative work.
- **Everytown for Gun Safety Support Fund**, a 501(c)(3), produces research, public education, convenings, and policy analysis.
- **Moms Demand Action** and **Students Demand Action** operate as Everytown-linked grassroots volunteer networks and appear repeatedly in state-level advocacy around these bills.

Everytown is not the only actor. Public sources also show roles for **Giffords**, **Brady**, **Hispanic Federation**, **state attorneys general**, **governors**, **district attorneys**, **local law enforcement officials**, and state-level gun-violence-prevention groups such as **Alliance for Gun Responsibility** in Washington.

The most concrete bill-specific evidence found so far:

- California AB 2047 committee analysis says the bill is sponsored by **Everytown for Gun Safety**.
- New York budget testimony was submitted by **Everytown for Gun Safety** in support of the governor's 3D-printed-firearm / DIY-machine-gun package.
- Washington HB 2320 was publicly supported by **Everytown**, **Moms Demand Action**, and **Students Demand Action**; Moms Demand Action said its volunteers testified, met lawmakers, and urged action.
- Colorado HB26-1144 was publicly promoted by Everytown and Students Demand Action, with Students Demand Action testimony in the official hearing packet.
- Minnesota SF 3661 had an Everytown support letter to the Senate Judiciary Committee.

## What "Funding The Push" Can Mean

There are at least four different money or influence paths:

1. **Direct bill sponsorship**: a legislature or committee record names an outside sponsor.
2. **Lobbying / testimony**: an organization submits testimony, sends action alerts, runs supporter emails, or meets lawmakers.
3. **Research and policy infrastructure**: reports, polling, model policy, convenings, and legal analysis that create the policy frame.
4. **Organization-level funding**: donors and revenue that support the organization generally, even if not traceable to a specific bill.

The public record is strongest for items 1 through 3. It is usually weaker for proving that a particular donor paid for a particular state bill.

## Everytown Ecosystem

### Public Role In The 3D-Printed Firearm Push

Everytown Support Fund released a 2025 white paper calling for urgent policy action on 3D-printed firearms. Its recommendations included printer manufacturers and software companies using algorithms to detect and block firearm/accessory printing, and lawmakers regulating 3D-printing technology so printers cannot be used to print guns.

Everytown also convened a 2025 3D-Printed Firearms Summit at Cornell Tech with policymakers, law enforcement, academics, private-sector leaders, and 3D-printing industry participants. The summit framed 3D-printed firearms as a rising public-safety threat and gave the policy push a national convening point.

### Public Funding And Structure

Everytown's own FAQ distinguishes the **Support Fund** as its 501(c)(3) education arm and the **Action Fund** as its 501(c)(4) advocacy and legislative arm. Its financial disclosure page links Form 990s and audited financials.

Cause IQ's 2024 summary lists Everytown for Gun Safety Action Fund as a 501(c)(4) with about **$57.1 million in total revenue** and about **$56.5 million in total expenses** for 2024. That is organization-level funding, not a bill-specific spend figure.

Everytown also has a federal PAC registered with the FEC as **Everytown for Gun Safety Action Fund, Inc. PAC** connected to the Action Fund.

Michael Bloomberg is central to the Everytown origin story. Bloomberg's public biography states that Mayors Against Illegal Guns joined with Moms Demand Action in 2014 to form Everytown for Gun Safety. Contemporary coverage reported that Bloomberg planned to spend up to **$50 million** through the new organization in 2014. Everytown later announced a **$45 million** 2024 electoral program through its Action Fund and Victory Fund focused on electing "gun sense" candidates.

### Important Limit

None of the organization-level funding sources above prove that any named donor paid for a specific 2026 state 3D-printer bill. They show the scale and funding structure of the advocacy ecosystem that is publicly supporting these policies.

## Other National Advocacy Groups

### Giffords

Giffords published a 2026 state trendwatch article tracking ghost-gun and DIY-firearm bills, including Colorado, New Jersey, Washington, Michigan, Minnesota, and California. Giffords Law Center says its 501(c)(3) receives funding from individual donors, foundations, law firms, and corporations.

Evidence found so far: policy tracking and public support for the general regulatory trend. Direct sponsorship of the 3D-printer-specific bills in this repo has not yet been found in the sources reviewed.

### Brady

Brady has supported related ghost-gun and 3D-printed-firearm measures, including California barrel/background-check legislation tied to 3D-printed firearms. This sweep did not find Brady as the named sponsor of the printer-blocking bills tracked here.

## Opposition And Counter-Advocacy To Track

The advocacy field is not one-sided. Public sources show opposition or concern from:

- **Electronic Frontier Foundation**, especially against printer-blocking mandates in New York and California, arguing they threaten privacy, speech, competition, and open-source tools.
- **California Rifle and Pistol Association** and other gun-rights organizations, listed in California committee analysis as opposing AB 2047.
- 3D-printing and maker communities, including open-source printer users and manufacturers concerned about firmware lock-in, feasibility, repairability, and liability.

This matters for funding research because the same method should be applied to both sides: identify named sponsors/supporters, then trace lobbying reports, campaign contributions, nonprofit filings, and PAC spending.
